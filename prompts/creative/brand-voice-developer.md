# Brand Voice Developer

---

## Description

Create comprehensive brand voice guidelines that define how your brand sounds across all touchpoints. This prompt helps marketers, brand managers, and content strategists develop clear, actionable voice documentation that teams can follow consistently.

---

## Use Cases

- Establishing brand voice for new companies or products
- Documenting existing brand personality for team alignment
- Refreshing brand voice during rebrands or pivots
- Creating voice guidelines for content teams and agencies
- Ensuring consistency across global or distributed teams

---

## 4. Voice Do's and Don'ts

### âœ… DO

**Be direct.**

- Get to the point fast
- Use short paragraphs and sentences
- Front-load the important information

**Sound human.**

- Use contractions (we're, you'll, it's)
- Say "you" and "your team"
- Write like you're talking to one person

**Show confidence.**

- Make statements, not hedges
- Use active voice
- Be specific with claims

**Add personality where appropriate.**

- Surprise with a clever turn of phrase
- Acknowledge shared frustrations
- Be a little unexpected

**Respect the reader's time.**

- If you can cut words, cut them
- Use bullets and headers for scanning
- Answer the question they're asking

---

### âŒ DON'T

**Don't use jargon and buzzwords.**

- No: synergy, leverage, utilize, facilitate, best-in-class
- No: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "cutting-edge"
- If you need a thesaurus, you're off track

**Don't be vague.**

- No: "Increase productivity" (how? by how much?)
- No: "Robust solution" (what does that even mean?)
- If it could apply to any product, rewrite it

**Don't be condescending.**

- No: "Simply click the button" (if it were simple, they wouldn't need help)
- No: "As you probably know..." (then why are you saying it?)
- Respect their intelligence

**Don't try too hard to be funny.**

- No: Excessive emojis or ALL CAPS
- No: Forced puns or dad jokes in serious contexts
- No: Humor that gets in the way of the message

**Don't hide bad news.**

- No: "We're making exciting changes!" (when things are being removed)
- If there's a problem, say it plainly
- Honesty builds trust

## 6. Channel-Specific Guidelines

### Website

- **Headlines:** Punchy, benefit-focused, personality welcome
- **Body copy:** Clear, scannable, support with specifics
- **CTAs:** Direct, action-oriented ("Start free" not "Get started today!")
- **Feature pages:** Lead with benefit, then explain how

### Blog

- **Headlines:** SEO-friendly but not boring
- **Tone:** More conversational, room for personality
- **Length:** Value completeness over brevity
- **POV:** "We" is fine; first-person for thought leadership

### Email

- **Subject lines:** Clear > clever (but clever + clear = ðŸŽ¯)
- **Body:** Get to the point in the first line
- **Length:** As short as possible while being complete
- **CTAs:** One primary CTA per email

### In-App / Product

- **Microcopy:** Crystal clear, minimal words
- **Errors:** Honest, helpful, not cute
- **Celebrations:** Brief, genuine ("Nice work!" not "ðŸŽ‰ AMAZING!!!")
- **Onboarding:** Patient, encouraging, show progress

### Social Media

- **LinkedIn:** Professional but not stiff, insights welcome
- **Twitter/X:** More casual, wit encouraged, engage genuinely
- **Replies:** Helpful first, personality second
- **Memes/trends:** Only if it fits naturally (forced = cringe)

### Help Docs

- **Tone:** Clear, patient, supportive
- **Structure:** Task-focused, easy to scan
- **Language:** Simplest possible, define terms if needed
- **No personality:** Clarity is king here
